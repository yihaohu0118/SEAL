Paper Poster BibTeX

Abstract

Large Language Model (LLM) agents are increasingly improved through interaction rather than static supervision. Yet most self-evolution methods adapt either the policy or the learning environment in isolation, leaving a structural gap: as the agent's capability frontier shifts during training, the environment that provides supervision often remains static or only weakly coupled to the agent's revealed failures. We call this mismatch Agent-Environment Misalignment.

We propose SEAL, a closed-loop co-evolution framework for interactive tool-use agents. SEAL collects on-policy trajectories under executable verification, diagnoses failed rollouts into turn-level labels, and uses these diagnoses as a shared signal for both environment-side adaptation and model-side policy optimization. Across in-distribution and out-of-distribution multi-turn tool-use evaluations, SEAL consistently improves low-resource agent learning, yielding +8.25 to +26.25 average-point gains across three backbones with only 400 training samples.

Method

SEAL treats the training-time environment as a learning interface: the agent still interacts with the same executable tools, rewards, task labels, and verifier, while the interface exposes more useful diagnostic signals during training.

Failed rollouts are converted into verifier-grounded turn-level labels, including invalid or missing tool calls, argument mismatches, recovery failures, and final-response mismatches. These diagnoses drive two coupled updates: interface evolution through schema cues and recovery feedback, and policy optimization through diagnosis-guided advantage reweighting.

Out-of-Distribution Transfer

Models trained only on the 400-sample BFCL V3 split transfer to held-out tool-use settings with different schemas, domains, and interaction patterns. SEAL improves both evaluated Qwen backbones on aggregate BFCL V4 and tau2-bench metrics, indicating that diagnosis-guided learning targets reusable tool-use behaviors rather than only memorizing the training distribution.
